# The environmental impact of the AI boom



Australia has more than [275](https://www.datacentermap.com/australia/) data centres, and the federal government is [actively pursuing](https://international.austrade.gov.au/en/do-business-with-australia/sectors/technology/ai-and-data-centres) the development of additional facilities, positioning the country as ‚Äú[the ideal location to build green data centres.](https://international.austrade.gov.au/en/do-business-with-australia/sectors/technology/ai-and-data-centres)‚Äù [Microsoft](https://news.microsoft.com/en-au/features/microsoft-announces-a5-billion-investment-in-computing-capacity-and-capability-to-help-australia-seize-the-ai-era/) and [Amazon](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/aws/amazon-data-center-investment-in-australia) have each announced major Australian data centre expansions, with Microsoft investing AUD 5 billion in 2023 and Amazon allocating AUD 20 billion for 2025‚Äì2029.



Data centres process, store, and distribute vast amounts of information and are essential for keeping the internet running. But AI is [reshaping](https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/ai-data-center#:~:text=AI%20models%20train%20and%20run,tensor%20processing%20Units%20(TPUs).) their role: AI models require enormous quantities of data to train and refine, and constant access to that data to respond to user queries. This surge in computational demand is driving [massive growth](https://www.jll.com/en-au/insights/market-outlook/data-center-outlook) in data centre construction.



Data centres have a tendency to guzzle large quantities of water in order to maintain safe operating temperatures, sometimes leaving entire communities [without water](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cy8gy7lv448o). Globally, AI infrastructure is [projected](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2304.03271) to use six times more water than the entire nation of Denmark. AI developers such as Amazon also have a [history](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2025/oct/25/amazon-datacentres-water-use-disclosure) of lying about how water is being used. For a country like Australia, long affected by drought, this raises serious concerns.



The energy burden is equally significant. In 2023, data centres consumed more electricity than Saudi Arabia and almost as much as France. A single data centre can consume enough electricity each year to power [50,000 homes](https://www.aph.gov.au/Parliamentary_Business/Committees/Senate/Adopting_Artificial_Intelligence_AI/AdoptingAI/Report/Chapter_6_-_Impacts_of_AI_on_the_environment). Experts have [warned](https://www.aph.gov.au/Parliamentary_Business/Committees/Senate/Adopting_Artificial_Intelligence_AI/AdoptingAI/Report/Chapter_6_-_Impacts_of_AI_on_the_environment) that Australia‚Äôs energy grid is ill-prepared for the rapid increase in demand driven by AI. Consuming this much energy results in huge carbon emissions. Indeed, projected data centre energy use has already [forced](https://www.afr.com/technology/australia-working-on-data-centre-strategy-but-risks-being-left-behind-20250924-p5mxil?) the Albanese government to soften its emissions reduction targets.



As Australia races to position itself as a hub for ‚Äúgreen‚Äù data, it must also reckon with the hidden costs of powering artificial intelligence. Without clear limits on energy and water use, the promise of innovation risks deepening the country‚Äôs environmental vulnerabilities.

# How is AI used to surveil Australian protests?



Melbourne Council has faced criticism over its [plan](https://www.abc.net.au/news/2025-10-08/melbourne-city-council-cctv-security-network-proposed-expansion/105864992) to install 100 new CCTV cameras with 60 of them privately owned. As the site of most major protests in Victoria, the council‚Äôs proposal raises concerns that the cameras could capture and identify protesters. The council has also indicated it [may](https://www.abc.net.au/news/2025-10-08/melbourne-city-council-cctv-security-network-proposed-expansion/105864992) use facial recognition on the footage, deepening privacy risks. Surveillance tools that collect biometric data and track protesters without consent expose political beliefs, enable disproportionate policing, and create a chilling effect that discourages democratic participation.



At the federal level, the Department of Home Affairs‚Äô contract with Babel Street grants [access](https://www.theguardian.com/australia-news/2023/nov/06/home-affairs-locate-x-paying-mobile-phone-tracking-tool) to Locate X, a smartphone tracking tool capable of identifying and tracking every device present at a protest. Home Affairs has justified its use of the tool by claiming it relies on ‚Äú[commercially and publicly available information](https://www.theguardian.com/australia-news/2023/nov/06/home-affairs-locate-x-paying-mobile-phone-tracking-tool),‚Äù exploiting a major loophole in Australian privacy law.



Meanwhile, private companies such as [Clearview AI](https://www.clearview.ai/federal) scrape images from protests to build facial recognition databases. Despite an [order](https://www.oaic.gov.au/news/media-centre/clearview-ai-breached-australians-privacy) from the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner requiring Clearview to cease operations in Australia and delete local data, there is [little evidence](https://www.oaic.gov.au/news/media-centre/statement-on-clearview-ai) it has complied.
